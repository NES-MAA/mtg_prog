Matthew Dogali

Worcester State University

Basic Needs Insecurity at Worcester State University

Quality access to food and housing is a widely spread problem for students at Worcester State University, and their affects creep into the classroom to impact students grades. This paper will define the overarching issue of basic needs insecurities, provide analysis of student data from Worcester State University, and suggest actions that will be taken to reduce the impact of these insecurities. Through this analysis, I will determine which demographics are impacted most and what level of impact basic needs insecurities have on grades. This paper will use three major statistical test, a Fisher Exact test, Linear Regression, and Pearson's Product Moment Correlation test. Similar analyses have been conducted through colleges throughout Massachusetts due to the prevalence and importance of basic needs insecurities. This paper will add another level of understanding to this issue, and provide support for programs to help. (Advisor: Jason Hardin)

Joseph Lindenmayer

Worcester State University

Testing the Efficiency of the Gambling Market for the NFL

The goal of this project is to identify if there is a reliable way to gamble on the results of NFL football games. Gambling sites and Vegas use a point spread and adjust according to public opinion in order to mitigate risk on each game. Using the efforts of both teams as explanatory variables, is it possible to predict the winner of the point spread before the game occurs? Considering statistics like offensive yards per game, turnovers or sacks can likely predict the winner and loser of a football game, it is anticipated that there is a regression model with a set of explanatory variables that should be able to profit in gambling against the point spread over the course of a season. (Advisor: Jason Hardin)

Subsection 4.2 Session II, ACC 105B

Samantha Mauro

Stonehill College

(with Sydney Martin, Stonehill College)

Minimal Tile and Bond-Edge Types for Self-Assembling DNA Graphs of Triangular Tiles

We examine self-assembling DNA from a graph theory perspective based on the tile model. These tiles represent junction branched molecules whose arms are double strands of DNA. We consider a family of graphs obtained by a string of connected triangles. Given a target graph G, a pot of tiles is designed in order to achieve G in three different scenarios corresponding to distinct levels of laboratory constraints. In the first scenario, graphs of a smaller size than G are allowed. In the second scenario, nonisomorphic graphs of the same size as G are allowed, but not graphs of smaller size. In the third scenario, only graphs isomorphic or larger in size to the target graph are allowed. We find the minimum number of tile types and bond-edge types for each of the three scenarios. (Advisors: Leyda Almodovar Velazquez & Heiko Todt)

How Ice Melts: Exploring Molecular Level Dynamics

Water is the only natural substance that exists in three fundamental states – solid, liquid, and gas at the normal range of temperatures found near the surface of earth. Enormous researches on water have been conducted. However, there is still a lot more to be understood at the nanoscale (molecular scale) level.

We present our study on the dynamics of water molecules in the process of water’s state change from ice (solid) to liquid water using a popular computer simulation method called Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulation. In this talk, we will introduce the mathematics behind the MD simulation and discuss some of the key analysis result from our work. (Advisor: Jane Lee)

Repunits and Arithmetic Progressions

There are many different aspects in mathematics that still need to be investigated further. This talk will focus around repunits and arithmetic progressions. Hannah Melanson

Keene State College

Repeating Integer Paradox

The decimal representation for the fraction \(1/3\) is \(0.3333\ldots = 0.\overline{3} \) where the three dots and the bar above the 3 each indicate that the threes continue without end. This repetition of digits can be done in the other direction to create \(\ldots3333\) represented by \(\overleftarrow{3}\text{.}\) This does not look like any natural number since there are infinitely many threes. In this presentation, we use some of the same techniques used in showing the relationship between \(1/3\) and \(\overline{3}\) along with some modular ten arithmetic to give meaning to \(\overleftarrow{3}\) and other repeating integers having this form. (Advisor: Vincent Ferlini)

6:15 PM
Samantha Sullivan

Keene State College

HS Sequences

A sequence of nonnegative integers begins with 2 followed by 3. The product is 6 and the sequence now becomes 2, 3, 6 . The next product 3×6=18 creates two values 1 and 8 and the sequence is extended to 2, 3, 6, 1, 8 . The next product 6×1=6 results in 2, 3, 6, 1, 8, 6 . The sequence will continue infinitely in this manner because each multiplication produces a single or double-digit nonnegative integer, so either one or two values will always be added on the “tail” of the sequence. This sequence first appeared in a problem devised by Hugo Steinhaus. A sequence generated in this way can begin with any two nonnegative single digits and it will be referred to as an HS sequence. This talk will analyze the long-term behavior of these sequences. (Advisor: Vincent Ferlini)

Broadening the Field: Charlotte Angas Scott and Women’s Mathematical Education

Charlotte Angas Scott (1858-1931) was a British algebraic geometer who spent her career at Bryn Mawr College in Pennsylvania. She was one of the first women to receive a PhD in mathematics, and she utilized her influence to create opportunities for the women who came after her. This paper studies Scott’s role in women’s higher education in mathematics through her programs at Bryn Mawr, as well as her role in developing the American mathematical community as a whole. Multivariate statistical models and their application to the study of adolescent alcohol use

Determining the Number of \(LU\) Decompositions of Matrices in \(M_2(\Z_n)\)

We expand the definition of \(LU\) decomposition to explore decompositions of matrices in \(M_2(\Z_n)\text{.}\) Focusing on matrices in \(M_2(\Z_n)\text{,}\) we determine a case when there are never any decompositions. Octal Games of Node-Kayles

Can two games with different rules really be the same game? The classic game of Nim can be generalized to a broader category of "taking and breaking" games called octal games. Cribbage: Counting Techniques, Partitions & Generating Functions

The card game cribbage has been used in the classroom as a way to enhance mental math skills at the K-12 and postsecondary levels. At the elementary level, educators can introduce their students to number sense by having them find pairs of cards that sum to fifteen, as obtaining fifteens is a crucial way to earn points in the game. Lauren Hartmann

Westfield State University

(with Emily Barranca, Swarthmore College & Clara Buck, Carleton College)

Special Sets of Vertices in Paley Graphs

Strongly regular graphs have three distinct eigenvalues that give bounds on the average degree of an induced subgraph. Tight sets are sets of vertices that induce a subgraph with as many or as few edges as possible relative to the parameters and eigenvalues of the graph. A particular family of examples of strongly regular graphs is the Paley Graph \(P(q)\text{,}\) which has the finite field of size \(q \equiv 1 \pmod{4}\) as its vertex set, where two vertices are adjacent when their difference is a nonzero perfect square in the considered field.

Motivated by a 1984 result by Blockhuis, we consider the connection between Paley graphs \(P(q^2)\) and finite affine planes. In this model, each line of the affine plane represents either a clique or an independent set in the graph. Selecting only those lines which correspond to cliques, we construct a partial affine plane in which two vertices are adjacent in the graph if and only if they are on the same line in the affine plane. This allows us to examine tight sets of \(P(q^2)\) in a geometric context. We work towards classifying the tight sets which are not the disjoint union of smaller tight sets. (Advisor: Morgan Rodgers, California State University, Fresno CA)
